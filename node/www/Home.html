Background

The RobotRun software was developed at Michigan Technological University as part of a collaborative initiative between MTU and Bay DeNoc Community College, and was made possible by a generous grant from the National Science Foundation under the project titled: University, Community College and Industry Partnership: Revamping Robotics Education to Meet 21st Century Workforce Needs (Grant #DUE-1501335). Its goal was to create a free, open-source software package for educational purposes. Robotic systems are expensive and often unavailable to High school and community college students. While simulations are a reasonable alternative, they can be very expensive. RobotRun is a completely free and open source simulator designed to let students learn to operate a robotic arm with out needing to own the arm itself. It is available for download here or the source may be downloaded and viewed here. Please note that RobotRun is designed for education and entertainment purposes only, and therefore does NOT include any production features found in enterprise-level software. RobotRun is not suitable for industrial use, and is not intended to replace any commercial software tools. RobotRemote is an extension of RobotRun started in the summer of 2018. It is a web-based system designed to allow people who have used the RobotRun software to test out what they have learned on a real robot. It allows users to remotely control the real robotic arm that the software was based on.

How it works

RobotRemote works by allowing the user to remotely view one of the robotic arms on the Michigan Tech campus, and control it using the interface on the RobotRun website. Two cameras are aimed at the robot itself allowing it to be viewed at multiple angles, while a third is a focused on the screen of the Teach Pendant used to control the robot. A set of buttons on the control panel screen made to replicate the Teach Pendant allows the user to input commands for the arm. Each of the buttons on the website when pressed sends a signal, a triggers an actuator to press the actual button on the Teach Pendant in the MTU robotics lab.
